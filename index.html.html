1930s — The Music Goes Home

Africa → America → Africa Again

Cuban rumba reaches the Congo. The circle closes. Something new is created: soukous.

Direct Link: Soundtrack of a Coup d'État

This circular journey — Africa to America and back — is exactly what the film documents with jazz. During the Cold War, American jazz traveled to Africa and became entangled with independence movements. Grimonprez's documentary asks: who owns this music? Who does it serve? The same question applies here with soukous and rumba.

Mid-20th Century — Barranquilla, Cartagena

The Picó: More Than a Speaker

Huge sound systems, Congolese records, and a culture that feels like home — because it is.

Soundtrack of a Coup d'État — The Political Power of Sound

Just as the film shows how jazz was weaponized in Cold War politics — used at the UN, played at independence celebrations in Leopoldville — the picó also carried political and social weight. It gave Black communities on Colombia's coast a space, an identity, and a voice. Sound as power. Both stories are the same story.

Final Reflection — Soundtrack of a Coup d'État

Grimonprez ends his film with an open question: can music change history? Our answer is that it already has — many times. From the navel dance in West Africa to the rumba in Havana to soukous in Kinshasa to a picó speaker in Barranquilla, something endured. That endurance is the subject of this episode. And it asks us the same question the film does: if you can hear where this music comes from, what responsibility does that carry?
